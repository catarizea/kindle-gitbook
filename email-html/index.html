3. Driving Vision with Outcomes
Highlight (yellow) - About Part II > Location 561
Traditionally, software projects are framed by requirements and deliverables. Teams are given requirements and are expected to produce deliverables that describe how the features that satisfy those requirements will look, behave, and perform. In many cases, the strategic context for those requirements is not communicated, is missing, or is simply not considered.
Highlight (yellow) - About Part II > Location 565
Lean UX radically shifts the way we frame our work by introducing back the strategic context for our feature and design choices and, more important, how we— the entire team, not just the design department— define success. Our goal is not to create a deliverable or a feature: it’s to positively affect customer behavior or change in the world— to create an outcome.
Highlight (yellow) - About Part II > Location 573
This reframing requires an organization- wide position of humility. It requires teams and managers to use their knowledge and skills and creativity as scientists might: they propose their best solution and then they test to see if they’re right.
Highlight (yellow) - About Part II > Location 578
It’s for these reasons that we begin with outcomes and assumptions instead of features and requirements.
Highlight (yellow) - Using the Right Words > Location 580
Language, in this case, is important. Requirements present a seemingly immutable path forward. Assumptions explicitly admit that we might be wrong. We use our assumptions to create and test hypotheses. If you’re familiar with Test- Driven Development (TDD), hypotheses are very similar. They are, in a way, a form of Test- Driven Product Design and Development. We write the test first— the hypothesis. We design and/ or develop just enough product (i.e., experiments and Minimum Viable Products [MVPs]) to see if the hypothesis is true.
Highlight (yellow) - Using the Right Words > Location 590
This chapter digs into the main tool of outcome- focused work: the hypothesis statement. The hypothesis statement is the starting point for a project. It states a clear strategic vision for the work and shifts the conversation between teams, managers, and stakeholders from outputs (e.g., “We will build an iPhone app”) to outcomes (e.g., “We want to increase the amount of commerce that comes through our mobile channels.”)

Highlight (yellow) - Design Systems > Location 1193

Highlight (yellow) - Monitoring Techniques for Continuous and Collaborative Discovery > Location 2061
A/ B testing is a technique, originally developed by marketers, to gauge which of two (or more) relatively similar concepts achieve the defined goal more effectively.

Highlight (pink) - Monitoring Techniques for Continuous and Collaborative Discovery > Location 2068
